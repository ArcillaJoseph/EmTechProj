“Transparency for a Better Philippines”

Predatory Governance, according to Rintel et al. (2022), is when leaders use traditional moral values to rise in power, but later shift focus to personal or factional agendas, distorting policies, weakening institutions, and eroding accountability.

It devalues governance rules and affects equity, trust in leadership, and the integrity of public service systems.

Is Predatory Governance Happening in the Philippines?

Sadly, yes. Over recent years, corruption cases and political scandals have become common headlines. Misuse of the national budget, shady deals, and selective justice are clear markers of this pattern.

As noted by Rintel and Fernandez (2020), even national agencies like PhilHealth have been riddled with fraud, especially during times when resources were most needed — like the pandemic.

Why Is It Happening?

Resource Theory explains this through the competition for scarce public goods in low-trust societies. Politicians exploit systems to secure loyalty or wealth, sidelining real service.

The logic of self-interest overrides civic duty, and without institutional checks, this culture spreads like wildfire.

What Can Be Done?

Transparency isn't just a buzzword — it's a weapon against corruption. Strengthening audit systems, digitalizing transactions, enforcing the Freedom of Information bill, and revisiting the 1987 Constitution are all vital.

Beyond laws, it's about citizens demanding more and holding leaders accountable. Every voice matters.

Conclusion

Governance should never be a tool for personal gain. The Philippines, rich in culture and talent, deserves better. By promoting transparency, integrity, and accountability, we light the way toward a fairer and stronger nation.
